Water on Paper

Every country has promised its people water. The promise is written down — in constitutions, missions, and master plans. This essay is about the distance between what the paper says and what comes out of the tap: from the whole planet, to India, to the screen you're reading this on.

01 — The scale

Almost none of Earth's water is ours to drink.

Picture all the water on Earth as a thousand drops. Here is where every one of them sits.

Saltwater — 975 drops Ice & glaciers — ~17 Fresh groundwater — ~7 Surface water we can reach — under 1

Of every thousand drops, about 975 are salt. Another 17 are frozen into ice caps and glaciers; roughly 7 more sit underground, much of it too deep or too old to lift. That leaves less than a single drop — the rivers and lakes we can actually reach — to carry almost all of human life.i

Scarcity isn't the surprising part. The surprising part is how good we've gotten at writing as if that one drop were poured out evenly — one promise per person, signed and filed. It is not. Where you are born decides how much reaches you, and whether it's safe when it arrives.

02 — On paper

On paper, everyone already has water.

In 2010 the UN General Assembly recognised water as a human right — 122 countries voted yes. The plans are real, ambitious, and beautifully written. The gap between them and a glass you'd let a child drink is the subject of everything that follows.

An aside · the business of thirst

When did water stop being free and start being a brand?

The same century that began writing water down as a right also learned to sell it back by the bottle. It took about two hundred years to turn a spring into a $300-billion market.

The ethics of it

A litre of premium bottled water can cost a thousand times what the tap charges — and much of it is drawn from springs and aquifers, sometimes in water-stressed ground, wrapped in plastic and shipped across the world. The purified brands are quieter still: Dasani and Aquafina are essentially filtered municipal tap water, sold back to the public that already owns it. Convenience is real. So is the question of who gets to fence off a human right.i

05 — When the pipe betrays you

Indore is India's cleanest city. In December, its water killed.

For eight years running, Indore won the title of cleanest city in India.i In late December 2025, residents of Bhagirathpura noticed their tap water had turned bitter and foul. With no real alternative, many kept drinking it.

0

people dead — including a six-month-old infant.

0

fell ill across one locality.

0

residents screened, door to door, after the fact.

The cause was almost banal: a public toilet had been built directly over a drinking-water pipeline, with no septic tank. Sewage seeped into the supply, which tested positive for bacteria found in human waste.i

A city can win every award for cleanliness and still lay a pipe beside a sewer.

Indore made the news because it was fast. The larger wound is slow.

Fifteen deaths on one street is the version that reaches television. Across the Ganga–Brahmaputra delta — West Bengal, Bihar, Assam and neighbouring Bangladesh — tens of millions draw water from shallow wells laced with naturally occurring arsenic. It is colourless and tasteless, and it poisons over years, not days — what the WHO called the largest mass poisoning of a population in history.i

No award, no headline, no single funeral — just a map drawn decades ago that safe piped water still hasn't caught up to. Indore is the wound you can see. This is the one you can't.

Indore isn't the outlier. It's the preview. Where are we heading?

The same forces — over-drawn aquifers, unmaintained pipes, cities growing past their supply — are queuing up elsewhere.

07 — The reckoning

Everything drinks water. This essay did too.

A web search. An AI prompt. A beer. A can of Diet Coke. Even the reverse-osmosis unit purifying your drinking water pours several litres down the drain for each clean one. So does every burger on the grill. The guilt is real — but guilt is a bad accountant, and not all water use is equal.

The real unit isn't the prompt. It's the building it runs in.

A single question is a few drops. But questions don't run alone — they run inside data centres that never switch off, and it's the building that drinks. Here is what one of them pulls in a single day, for cooling alone.

A typical data centre
~1.1M litres / day

The everyday water of about 1,000 households — drawn by one building, every single day.

A large one
~19M litres / day

A whole town of up to 50,000 people — the thirst of one hyperscale campus.i

Now multiply one building across thousands of sites, running around the clock — often on drinking-quality water, increasingly built in already-stressed basins. Globally, data centres consumed an estimated 560 billion litres in 2023, heading past 1,200 billion by 2030 — more than the entire city of London drinks in a year.i

Your own share, though, is a rounding error.

The building is the story — not you. Put a whole year of one person's daily AI prompts next to an ordinary day, in litres, and it barely clears the axis.

A 100-megawatt AI facility can draw around 2 million litres a day while pulling more electricity than 75,000 homes, and its cooling towers evaporate roughly 80% of what they take — gone from the local basin until it falls again as rain, somewhere else. Even cleaning water costs water: a traditional home reverse-osmosis unit sends 3–4 litres to the drain for every clean litre it makes.i

A litre is not a litre — four things decide if it matters.

Quality
Potable vs. recycled

Evaporating treated drinking water in a thirsty city is worlds apart from a closed loop on grey water.

Place
Where it's drawn

A litre in rain-soaked Kerala isn't a litre in arid Rajasthan. Stressed ground can't spare it.

Fate
Returned vs. gone

Withdrawn-and-returned water re-enters the system. Evaporated water leaves the basin entirely.

Honesty
Measured vs. guessed

"One prompt = a bottle" is a shortcut. Real intensity varies up to 10,000× by site.

AI isn't the whole picture. But we need water, policy and regulation before we need more data centres.

The point isn't to stop searching, or building, or drinking a beer. It's the order of priorities. Before a stressed basin is opened up for one more thirsty industry — before more of a resource that already isn't reaching people is spent — the paper has to become the pipe. Measure honestly, build where the rain is, and provide before you expand. The fix for AI's thirst turns out to be the fix for everything in this story.
